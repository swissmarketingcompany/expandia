# Go Expandia Presentation Plan

Prepared on April 3, 2026.

Assumption: this is a customer-facing company presentation for B2B prospects who already use public AI tools but have not yet turned AI into a structured operating capability.

## 1. Business Model Synthesis

Based on the current site source, Go Expandia's model is much clearer than a generic "AI consultancy" pitch.

- Main offer: AI support for companies.
- Core promise: move companies from public AI tool usage to real business use.
- Service ladder: AI Opportunity Review, AI Plan, AI Build & Setup, AI Training, AI Support.
- Commercial model: low-friction fixed-fee entry, then project or monthly expansion.
- Buyer outcomes: more revenue, lower cost, better cash flow, stronger operational control.
- Process logic: business value first, then workflow design, then build, then adoption, then support.
- Trust logic: privacy boundaries first, NDA, transparent analysis, live demos, practical delivery.

What this means for the deck:

- The presentation should sell a clear starting point, not "everything AI."
- The real hero is not the build stage. It is the structured path from uncertainty to implementation.
- The strongest CTA is "start with the review" because it reduces buyer risk and matches the site's land-and-expand model.

## 2. Strategic Strengths To Highlight

- Plain-English offer in a crowded market full of vague AI language.
- Clear 5-step service ladder that makes the buying journey understandable.
- Strong entry offer with visible pricing, which lowers decision friction.
- Business-first framing instead of tool-first framing.
- Adoption is treated as part of delivery, not as an afterthought.
- Support and monthly work create a natural recurring revenue path after initial projects.

## 3. Messaging Risks To Control

These are not fatal, but they matter for a presentation because they can dilute the story.

- The current contact page still frames the company as a broad IT, AI, and software partner instead of the tighter "AI Support for Companies" offer.
- Some blog and metadata content still reflects older or broader service lines, including marketing, sales, and managed IT themes.
- I did not find public case studies, customer logos, or hard outcome proof in the current site source.
- Team credibility is implied, but the presentation will need visible human authority: founders, operators, delivery leads, or advisors.

Presentation implication:

- Keep the deck tightly aligned to the current AI support model.
- Do not lean on the broader "business tech stack" message unless you are preparing a separate parent-company or partner deck.
- If proof is thin, use de-identified workflow examples and sample deliverables instead of unsupported ROI claims.

## 4. What Current Market Research Says

### 4.1 Market timing is real, but buyers are still early in operational maturity

- McKinsey's 2025 State of AI says 78% of organizations use AI in at least one business function, up from 72% in early 2024 and 55% a year earlier.
- The same McKinsey report says 71% regularly use generative AI in at least one business function.
- But only 21% say they have fundamentally redesigned at least some workflows around gen AI.

Why this matters for Go Expandia:

- The category is no longer "why AI?"
- The real sales message is "how to turn isolated use into workflow-level value."

### 4.2 Workflow redesign matters more than AI enthusiasm

- McKinsey reports that, across 25 tested attributes, redesigning workflows had the biggest effect on an organization's ability to see EBIT impact from gen AI.
- Accenture's 2025 report similarly argues that the real power of AI comes when companies reinvent work routines end to end, not when they layer AI on top of legacy processes.

Why this matters for Go Expandia:

- The deck should frame Go Expandia as a workflow-and-implementation partner, not a prompt trainer or generic strategy shop.

### 4.3 The biggest blockers are data, governance, and adoption

- KPMG's AI Quarterly Pulse Survey on January 9, 2025 found that 85% of leaders named data quality as the biggest challenge to AI strategy in 2025.
- The same KPMG survey found 71% cited data privacy and cybersecurity, and 46% cited employee adoption.
- Deloitte's 2026 State of AI in the Enterprise says only one in five companies has a mature governance model for autonomous AI agents.

Why this matters for Go Expandia:

- The review should be positioned as a way to reduce bad starting assumptions.
- Privacy boundaries, safe handling, and transparent process should be on-slide, not buried in Q&A.
- Training and support should be presented as core value drivers, not add-ons.

### 4.4 Buyers want value, but they still struggle to prove ROI

- KPMG reported on January 9, 2025 that half of leaders were scaling GenAI, but only 31% expected to measure ROI in the next six months, and none said they had already reached that stage.
- Deloitte's 2026 report says 66% of organizations are seeing productivity gains, 40% cost reduction, and 38% better customer relationships, but only 20% report revenue growth so far.

Why this matters for Go Expandia:

- The presentation should promise measurable operational gains first.
- Revenue upside should be included, but it should not be the only story.

### 4.5 Skills and adoption are now business issues, not HR side notes

- PwC's 2025 Global AI Jobs Barometer says industries most exposed to AI are seeing 3x higher growth in revenue per employee.
- That report also says workers with AI skills command a 56% wage premium on average.
- PwC also reports that skills sought by employers are changing 66% faster in the most AI-exposed occupations.

Why this matters for Go Expandia:

- Training deserves its own serious slide.
- "Permanent adoption" is not a soft message. It is part of value capture.

### 4.6 The market is moving toward agents and embedded AI, but most firms are not ready

- Microsoft's Work Trend Index, published April 23, 2025, says 82% of leaders view 2025 as a pivotal year to rethink strategy and operations.
- The same report says 81% expect agents to be moderately or extensively integrated into company AI strategy within 12-18 months.
- KPMG's January 9, 2025 survey found 51% of organizations were exploring AI agents and 37% were piloting them, but only 12% had deployed them.

Why this matters for Go Expandia:

- The deck should show that Go Expandia can help buyers move from tool usage to practical operating workflows.
- It should not over-index on "agents" as a buzzword. It should show ordered progression.

## 5. Competitive Framing

Inference from the market sources:

- Large consultancies sell enterprise transformation, governance, and scale.
- Platform vendors sell software, infrastructure, and ecosystem lock-in.
- Many smaller agencies sell AI experimentation, workshops, or automations without a structured operating model.

Where Go Expandia can win:

- Simpler entry point than enterprise consultancies.
- More operational and delivery-oriented than strategy-heavy advisors.
- More business-guided than tool-first builders.
- More adoption-aware than pure software shops.

Best positioning sentence:

> Go Expandia helps B2B companies move from public AI tools to real business use through a clear five-step path: review, plan, build, train, and support.

## 6. Presentation Goal

Primary goal:

- Convert a skeptical but interested B2B prospect into a call or paid AI Opportunity Review.

Secondary goal:

- Show that Go Expandia is not a one-off workshop provider. It is a partner for structured AI rollout and ongoing improvement.

## 7. Recommended Audience

Inferred best-fit audience:

- CEO
- COO
- Head of Operations
- RevOps or Sales Operations leader
- Customer operations or service leader
- IT or transformation leader at a B2B company

Inferred best-fit company profile:

- Already uses ChatGPT, Gemini, or similar public tools.
- Feels internal pressure to "do something with AI."
- Has fragmented data, manual workflows, and unclear ownership.
- Wants a practical first move without committing to a massive transformation program.

## 8. Recommended Deck Structure

Target length: 10-12 core slides, plus appendix.

### Slide 1: Cover

Message:

- AI Support for Companies
- From public AI tools to real business use

Goal:

- Establish the category in plain English.

### Slide 2: Why This Matters Now

Message:

- AI adoption is rising fast, but most companies still have not redesigned workflows or captured full value.

Suggested proof:

- McKinsey adoption numbers.
- Microsoft 2025 urgency numbers.

### Slide 3: The Buyer Problem

Message:

- Most companies have AI activity, not an AI operating model.
- Tools are being used, but data, ownership, security, and adoption are still broken.

Suggested proof:

- KPMG blocker data.

### Slide 4: The Go Expandia Thesis

Message:

- Data first. Build second. Adoption always.
- Business value before tool selection.

Goal:

- Introduce the core belief system behind the offer.

### Slide 5: The 5-Service Model

Message:

- Review -> Plan -> Build & Setup -> Training -> Support

Goal:

- Make the path easy to understand and easy to buy.

Visual:

- A simple left-to-right service ladder.

### Slide 6: Start Small, Reduce Risk

Message:

- You do not need to buy everything at once.
- Start with the review if the next step is unclear.

Suggested proof:

- Fixed-fee entry pricing.

### Slide 7: What the Review Actually Produces

Message:

- A shortlist of high-value AI opportunities tied to revenue, cost, cash flow, and control.

Important:

- If possible, show an anonymized sample output or mock page from the review.

### Slide 8: From Findings to Working Plan

Message:

- The plan decides what to build now, what to stage later, and what should be project work versus monthly work.

Goal:

- Show commercial clarity, not just technical planning.

### Slide 9: Delivery and Adoption

Message:

- Build, train, and support are part of one operating model.
- AI only matters when the team can use it in daily work.

Suggested proof:

- PwC skills data.
- Deloitte adoption/governance gap.

### Slide 10: Why Go Expandia

Message:

- Business-first
- Clear order
- Real delivery
- Transparent analysis
- Practical privacy boundaries

Goal:

- Differentiate from generic consultants and tool vendors.

### Slide 11: Example Use Cases or Outcome Paths

Message:

- Show 3-4 de-identified examples by business outcome:
- Revenue
- Cost
- Cash flow
- Control

Example format:

- Problem
- AI opportunity
- What Go Expandia would review/build/train/support

### Slide 12: Call to Action

Message:

- Start with one review, one plan, one build, one training session, or ongoing support.

Recommended CTA:

- Book the AI Opportunity Review

## 9. Appendix Slides To Prepare

- Privacy, NDA, and data-handling process
- Sample project timeline
- Sample review deliverable
- Commercial model and buying paths
- Team/founder credentials
- FAQ: existing tools, security, training, support, ROI expectations

## 10. What To Avoid In The Presentation

- Generic "AI is the future" slides without buyer-specific meaning
- Long lists of tools or model names
- Broad IT-services messaging that weakens the AI support narrative
- Claims about proprietary systems that cannot be explained clearly
- Big ROI promises without proof
- Trying to sell all five services equally hard on every slide

## 11. Assets You Should Gather Before Building The Deck

- Founder or lead operator bios
- Short company story and origin
- One anonymized review output example
- One anonymized implementation workflow example
- One training/adoption example
- One live-demo or system screenshot
- Any client quote, testimonial, or reference you are allowed to use
- A simple pricing snapshot
- Data privacy and NDA flow

## 12. Best Narrative Angle

Recommended one-line narrative:

- Most companies have started using AI. Very few have turned it into a practical business system. Go Expandia gives them a clear first step and a clear path from review to adoption.

Recommended title for the deck:

- Go Expandia: From Public AI Tools to Real Business Use
